# Accelerating LLM Training in Optical AI Clusters with Asynchronously-Invoked Hitless In-Job Partial TPE

Yuxuan Dou<sup>1</sup> , Zeyu Li<sup>1</sup> , Xuexia Xie<sup>1</sup> , Xiaoliang Chen<sup>1</sup> , and Zuqing Zhu<sup>1</sup>

*<sup>1</sup>University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei, Anhui 230027, China, xlichen@ieee.org, zqzhu@ieee.org*

Abstract: We propose a generic framework for an optical AI cluster to perform in-job partial reconfiguration of optical circuit switches across communication phases in LLM training. By dynamically adapting the inter-rack topology to time-varying traffic matrix of training, our proposal enhances the utilization of otherwise idle optical connections and thus effectively accelerates GPT-2 training.

### 1. Introduction

Recently, the massive computing demand by large language model (LLM) training has presented a major barrier to the rapid technological advances of LLM. However, when the artificial intelligence (AI) communities are continuously devoted to expanding their budgets on computing power [1, 2], sustaining effective LLM training over large numbers of GPUs/TPUs is extremely challenging, primarily bottlenecked by communication bandwidth [3]. Specifically, LLM training typically employs multi-dimensional parallelism (mixture of data, pipeline, tensor and expert parallelisms), which synchronizes through collective communications to produce high-volume and spatially-and-temporally skewed traffic patterns [2]. Therefore, with the availability of high-port-count optical circuit switches (OCS'), optical interconnects have been viewed as cornerstones of AI clusters [4]. By replacing power-hungry and capacity-limited electrical packet switches (EPS') with a flat plane of rate-oblivious OCS' (Fig. 1(a)), optical AI clusters offer direct connectivity among racks/pods and largely improve network performance and scalability. Moreover, OCS' can be dynamically reconfigured to form various topologies, also known as topology engineering (TPE) [5], to adapt to the spatially-skewed traffic generated by LLM training, and the merits of TPE for LLM training have been confirmed recently [6, 7].

Nevertheless, there is no free lunch, and the efficiency and adaptivity of optical AI clusters with TPE are restricted by the latency of OCS reconfiguration. Specifically, commercial OCS' normally require tens of milliseconds or more to be reconfigured and become stabilized afterward, while such a latency is comparable to the duration of a communication phase in LLM training [1]. This almost rules out the feasibility of invoking in-job TPE (*i.e.*, TPE within training in runtime) to adapt an inter-rack/pod topology to the instant traffic matrix of LLM training. As shown in Fig. 1(b), there are normally multiple communication phases, each of which can produce a distinct traffic matrix, in a training iteration, when multi-dimensional parallelism is used for LLM training. The time-varying traffic pattern will become even more complicated when multiple training jobs run concurrently. Hence, existing approaches mostly invoke TPE in a coarse time granularity, *i.e.*, computing a one-for-all TPE for being used throughout the training of a batch of jobs [1, 6, 7]. However, as the traffic matrix of LLM training is time-varying and can change significantly (even when there is only one training job, as exemplified in Fig. 1(b)), such a one-for-all TPE can hardly keep pace with the traffic changes, leading to the mismatch that will prolong communication time. This calls for in-job TPE optimization, which was tackled in [8] by by hiding the latency of a TPE-induced OCS reconfiguration behind the subsequent computing phase. Nevertheless, this requires perfect separation of computing and communication phases in each training iteration, which is usually not the case in LLM training using multi-dimensional parallelism [2], thereby limits its practicalness.

In this work, we resolve the limitations above by proposing a generic in-job TPE optimization framework, namely, *Hitless In-job Partial TPE (HIP-TPE)*. Specifically, HIP-TPE spatially divides OCS ports into two groups, one on the rails while the other underpins in-job TPE targeted for future traffic demands. Based on predicted inter-rack/pod traffic matrices (*e.g.*, fetched from the collective communication library of LLM training), HIP-TPE schedules several *stages* in advance, each of which contains one or more communication phases of training job(s) and possesses a disparate traffic profile with duration comparable to the OCS reconfiguration time. In each stage, HIP-TPE identifies non-critical OCS ports to put in the reconfigurable group and reconfigures them to prepare for the traffic demands in the next stage. In this way, HIP-TPE realizes the in-job TPE optimization that is asynchronous to training iterations and minimizes the impact of TPE on in-service traffic (*i.e.*, hitless partial TPE is achieved). We prototyped HIP-TPE on a small-scale but real AI cluster testbed and demonstrated to reduce the average iteration time of GPT-2 training by up to 12.4%.

Each  $d_{u,v}\in D^t$  and  $e_{u,v}\in E^t$   $(u,v\in V)$  respectively denote the volume of data transfer and number of connections established (upper-bounded by  $K\times |W|$ ) for the communication from u to v at stage t. To explain the necessity of in-job TPE optimization, we exemplify an LLM training with multi-dimensional parallelism over 8 racks and profile its time-varying traffic matrix in Fig. 1(b), which indicates that there are 4 communication phases with disparate patterns in each training iteration. For instance, Rack 7 talks intensively with Racks 3 and 5 in Phase 3 but switches abruptly to Rack 6 in Phase 4. Hence, the one-shot TPE calculated on the composite traffic matrix cannot always match to the instant traffic matrix, inevitably resulting in connection under-utilization across phases (see the bottom of Fig. 1(d)). For simplicity, HIP-TPE can just map each phase in Fig. 1(b) to a stage to realize in-job TPE optimization (see the top of Fig. 1(d)). In a generic scenario with multiple concurrent jobs, HIP-TPE identifies disparate traffic patterns to organize the jobs' communication phases into stages, which can be asynchronous to their iterations.

HIP-TPE realizes in-job TPE optimization for LLM training job(s) (J) by dual OCS port grouping and partial TPE, following the flow chat in Fig. 1(c). (**Step 1**) We profile the collective communications of J to get a series of stages with distinctive traffic matrices  $\{D^t|_t\}$  and projected data transfer time comparable to the OCS reconfiguration time. This is doable with the information from the training scheduler. (**Step 2**) In each stage t, a subset of OCS ports that are currently on the rails but less critical for  $D^t$  are identified as the reconfigurable group  $\hat{W}^t$  for the next stage. Then, we plan TPE  $G^{t+1}$  for  $D^{t+1}$  by planning ports in  $\hat{W}^t$  as a complement to the unchanged ports ( $W \setminus \hat{W}^t$ ), using a well-designed TPE algorithm. (**Step 3**) The ports in  $\hat{W}^t$  are then reconfigured without affecting LLM training as the traffic on them are rerouted in the make-before-break manner. (**Step 4**) We compute and pre-configure a new set of routing paths for carrying  $D^{t+1}$  in  $G^{t+1}$ . (**Step 5**) Upon the arrival of stage t+1, we activate the new routing entries in top-of-rack (ToR) switches (can be done with negligible time) to seamlessly switch them to use connections offered by  $G^{t+1}$ . Afterward, we repeat Steps 2-4 to always get the optical AI cluster prepared for the next stage with hitless partial TPE.

The top part of Fig. 1(d) showcases the procedure of HIP-TPE to serve the LLM training in Fig. 1(b), which can customize the inter-rack topology for each stage and reduces the communication time per iteration from 31.5 seconds

(one-shot TPE) to 10.5 seconds. Meanwhile, HIP-TPE improves the connection utilization from 50% to 75%.

#### 3. Experimental Demonstrations

We prototyped HIP-TPE on a real-world 8-node optical AI cluster testbed running GPT-2 training jobs. Fig. 2(a) shows the testbed setup, where each node (i.e., a GPU server) has an NVIDIA GeForce GTX 1080 Ti GPU and connects to a ToR switch (realized by a Pica8 switch) through a 10-Gbps port. The ToR switches are interconnected by an OCS (a Polatis 24×24 optical matrix switch), whose reconfiguration time is ~50 milliseconds. Each ToR switch wires to the OCS through three duplex optical ports operating at 1 Gbps. Here, we purposely limited the port rate to 1 Gbps to emulate the unmatched bandwidth setting in today's optical AI clusters (i.e., the total capacity between a ToR switch and its GPU servers is much larger than that between the ToR switch and the OCS plane) [1]. We deployed the Megatron-DeepSpeed platform and run GPT-2-M pretraining jobs with it. Three model sizes were considered, i.e., with 100 million (M), 300 M and 1 billion (B) parameters, and the training data volume is 1 GB for all of them. A three-dimensional parallelism with parallelization degree of [TP = 2, PP = 2, DP = 2] was configured for the training. We benchmarked HIP-TPE against one-shot TPE. Here, to ensure fair comparisons, we use the same algorithm to calculate TPE based a traffic matrix, i.e., HIP-TPE uses the algorithm to get TPE  $G^{t+1}$  based on  $D^{t+1}$  and  $\hat{W}^t$ , while one-shot TPE obtains TPE based on the composite traffic matrix covering a whole training iteration with it.

Fig. 2(b) visualizes the 5 traffic matrices that we observed in each training iteration of GPT-2-M. They exhibit high spatial sparsity and skewness, with 12 or less flows running concurrently for totally 28 node pairs. We also plotted the inter-rack topologies optimized by HIP-TPE in Fig. 2(b), which presents clear differences. To quantity the benefits of in-job TPE, we show the training curves of both approaches over around an hour of training in Fig. 2(c). While they both facilitate correct training to reduce the loss function gradually, HIP-TPE achieves a faster training speed (e.g., saving  $\sim$ 640 seconds to reduce the loss below 8.4) since its communication time in each iteration is shorter. Fig. 2(d) compares the average training iteration time with the two approaches (averaged over 200 iterations), which indicates that HIP-TPE consistently outperforms one-shot TPE and its advantage enlarges as the model scales up (up to 12.4% reduction). This is because the volume of data transfers increases with the model size and thus a larger model poses a greater demand for in-job TPE. Fig. 2(e) shows the average link bandwidth utilization and peak rate during training, and HIP-TPE increases the bandwidth utilization from 38% to 46% while improves the peak rate by 147%.

#### 4. Summary

This work proposed HIP-TPE to enable partial OCS reconfiguration in runtime for accelerating LLM training in an optical AI cluster. We prototyped HIP-TPE in a real-world AI cluster testbed, and our experimental results indicated that it reduced the average training iteration time of GPT-2 by up to 12.4% over the conventional one-shot TPE.
