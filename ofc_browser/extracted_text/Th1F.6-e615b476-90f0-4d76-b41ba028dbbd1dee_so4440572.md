# A photonic integrated mode-locked laser based on dispersion-managed mode-locking architecture

Xurong Li,1,2 Zheru Qiu,1,2 Xuan Yang, 1,2 Xinru Ji,1,2 Jiale Sun,1,2 Jianqi Hu,1,2 Grigorii Likhachev,1,2 Ulrich Kentsch,<sup>3</sup> and Tobias J. Kippenberg1,2,\*

*1 Institute of Physics, Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Lausanne (EPFL), CH-1015 Lausanne, Switzerland 2 Institute of Electrical and Micro Engineering, Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Lausanne (EPFL), CH-1015 Lausanne, Switzerland <sup>3</sup> Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf (HZDR), 01328 Dresden, Germany \* tobias.kippenberg@epfl.ch*

Abstract: We demonstrate the first self-starting, photonic integrated femtosecond modelocked laser, using a dispersion-managed mode-locking architecture. The laser has a 1.2- GHz repetition rate with an ultra-low mode-locking threshold, and generates a passively stable optical frequency comb. © 2025 The Author(s)

## 1. Introduction

Femtosecond mode-locked lasers (MLLs) are essential tools in numerous areas, from high-precision optical frequency metrology, tracking chemical reaction dynamics, time distribution, to corneal surgery [\[1\]](#page-2-0). While solidstate and fiber MLLs still offer the best performance, miniaturized femtosecond MLLs are attractive for their better size, weight, and power (SWaP) as well as lower cost. However, current integrated ultrafast sources still fall significantly short of the performance compared to the bench-top counterparts. Kerr soliton microcombs have repetition rates from tens of GHz to THz range and usually have low conversion efficiency [\[2\]](#page-2-1). Integrated electrooptic comb generators can produce broadband spectra, but require external microwave sources [\[2\]](#page-2-1). Active MLLs on thin-film lithium niobate have been demonstrated, while producing only picosecond pulses and requiring external microwave driving [\[3\]](#page-2-2). Integrated semiconductor MLLs typically only generate picosecond pulses with poor noise performance [\[4\]](#page-2-3). Rare-earth-doped waveguide MLLs have so far been limited to pulse energies around 5 pJ with a relatively large footprint in planar lightwave circuits [\[5\]](#page-2-4). Recent efforts to integrate these systems in photonic integrated circuits (PICs) have only yield Q-switching [\[6\]](#page-2-5) or Q-switched mode-locking [\[7\]](#page-2-6) operations. The high nonlinearity associated with high-energy optical pulses confined in nano-scale waveguides can lead to wave breaking [\[8\]](#page-2-7) and make passive mode-locking in PICs challenging.

Here, we demonstrate the first turnkey photonic integrated passive MLL, based on a dispersion-managed modelocking architecture. The MLL consists of three main components: erbium-doped silicon nitride (Er:Si3N4) waveguides, chirped Bragg gratings (CBGs), and a semiconductor saturable absorber mirror (SESAM). It can reach stable mode-locking state at a pump power of only 27.3 mW, and generate optical pulses with repetition rate of 1.2 GHz and pulse duration of 300 fs. The ultra-low mode-locking threshold enables turnkey operation, deterministic multi-soliton generation, and battery operation of a compact MLL system. It provides exceptional comb-line stability, two-order-of-magnitude better than that of a commercial fiber MLL, and superior noise performance, three-order-of-magnitude better than that of the best-reported integrated MLL [\[9\]](#page-2-8).

## 2. Dispersion-managed mode locking

In dispersion-managed mode-locking architecture, normally-dispersive and anomalously-dispersive sections are distributed along the cavity, resulting optical pulses to alternately stretch and compress as they circulate inside the cavity and reducing the averaged peak power and nonlinearity. In our MLL, the normal-dispersion Er:Si3N<sup>4</sup> waveguides, which are more immune to pulse breaking [\[8\]](#page-2-7), provide optical gain. The CBGs simultaneously serve as a cavity mirror, an output coupler, a pump injector, and a dispersion compensator, offering net-cavity anomalous dispersion to support dispersion-managed soliton [\[10\]](#page-2-9). A SESAM, which is commercially available, initiates mode-locking by suppressing low-intensity noises and reflecting high-intensity pulses.

Our MLLs are fabricated on a 200-nm Si3N<sup>4</sup> PIC platform on 4-inch silicon wafers with wafer-scale erbium ion implantation. The MLL chip only occupies a 5 × 5-mm<sup>2</sup> area with 12 individual MLLs and ∼ 7-cm cavity lengths. CBGs have 2000-3000 grating periods, linearly chirped to cover Bragg wavelengths from 1500 to 1600

The MLL can generate stable pulse train at a 1.2-GHz repetition rate (Fig. [1e](#page-1-0)), with on-chip output power up to 22.5 mW and the 3-dB bandwidth up to 9.5 nm (Fig. [1f](#page-1-0)). The minimum full-width-halfmaximum is 424.7 fs, corresponding to a 300-fs pulse width for a Gaussian-type pulse (Fig. [1g](#page-1-0)). The MLL can reach stable mode-locking state at only 27.3-mW pump power by incorporating pump-recycling gratings at the end of the Er:Si3N<sup>4</sup> waveguide before the SESAM port. The ultra-low mode-locking threshold enables a compact MLL system powered by only 4 AA batteries.

# 3. Passively stable optical frequency comb

The regular pulse train generated by our photonic integrated MLL (PIC-MLL) corresponds to an optical frequency comb in the spectral domain. To validate its comb nature, we heterodyne-beat our MLL with a frequency-stabilized reference laser, and compare it with a commercial fiber MLL (Menlo ELMO) in the same experiment (Fig. [2a](#page-2-10),b). While the comb line of commercial fiber MLL drifts more than 33 repetition rates within 1 hour, our PIC-MLL exhibits exceptional comb-line stability: the standard deviation of the comb-line drift during the 2-hour measurement is only 8.0 MHz, corresponding to less than 1% of the repetition rate. Allan deviations of the comb-line drift and comb-line-number-scaled repetition rate further suggest that the PIC-MLL has two-order-of-magnitude higher comb-line stability than the commercial fiber MLL at an averaging time of 1000 s (Fig. [2c](#page-2-10)).
