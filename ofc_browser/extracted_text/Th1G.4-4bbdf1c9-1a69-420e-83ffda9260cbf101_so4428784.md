# Multicore Fiber Coupled Backside-Emitting VCSEL/PD Arrays for High-Bandwidth Optical Interconnects in Data Center

Weipeng Zhang,1,\* and Manchen Hu,<sup>1</sup>

*<sup>1</sup>LightXcelerate Inc., 555 Bryant St Suite 406, Palo Alto, CA 94301, USA \*weipeng@lightxcelerate.com*

Abstract: We present a 19-core-MCF-coupled co-packaged optical chiplet integrating backside-emitting VCSEL and backside-illuminated PD arrays flip-bonded to driver–TIA EICs. Fundamental optical performance and thermal-path feasibility are demonstrated, with envisioned scalability toward multi-chiplet GPU optical interconnects.

## 1. Introduction

AI and ML clusters now comprise thousands of GPUs requiring multi-Tb/s interconnects. Electrical links beyond 100 Gb/s per lane face excessive loss, crosstalk, and equalization overhead, forming a practical "bandwidth wall" that limits scaling and drives interconnect power to unsustainable levels. Co-packaged optics (CPO) alleviates these bottlenecks by moving optical I/O adjacent to compute packages, shortening electrical reach while improving bandwidth density and energy efficiency. For realistic GPU integration, optical engines must achieve high bandwidth and low energy per bit with manufacturable, thermally robust packaging.

Among competing technologies, silicon photonics (SiPh) represents the dominant "fast-and-narrow" strategy [\[1\]](#page-2-0). Wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) using microring resonators enables large per-fiber throughput, but the resonant nature of the devices requires tight wavelength control and continuous thermal tuning. The need for external continuous-wave lasers, complex alignment, and hermetic packaging complicates SiPh integration with high-power GPU packages.

MicroLED-based interconnects have emerged as an alternative "wide-and-slow" approach [\[2\]](#page-2-1). Their direct electrical modulation eliminates external lasers and thermal tuning, enabling compact and temperature-robust operation. However, material and carrier-lifetime limits restrict their modulation speed (typically <5 Gb/s) and optical efficiency (<10%), making it difficult to scale toward multi-Tb/s GPU systems. Reliability and thermal stress under continuous high-current drive also remain open issues.

Vertical-cavity surface-emitting lasers (VCSELs) provide a more mature, manufacturable realization of the wide-and-slow concept [\[3\]](#page-2-2). VCSELs have demonstrated >100 Gb/s modulation, 10–20% wall-plug efficiency, and proven reliability across billions of datacenter transceivers. These attributes make VCSEL arrays the most practical and low-risk light sources for short-reach optical I/O. Extending VCSEL technology from traditional pluggable modules to dense 2D chiplets enables a scalable and cost-effective CPO platform directly compatible with GPU packaging.

The key challenge lies in adapting VCSELs to high-density integration. Conventional front-emitting VCSELs route both electrodes and optical emission through the same surface, favoring wire-bond assembly but hindering flip-chip packaging. Dense optical engines require short interconnects to drivers and TIAs, making front-emitting structures impractical without transparent interposers. Backside-emitting (BSE) VCSELs solve this problem by placing all electrodes on the bottom while emitting upward through the substrate, inherently supporting flip-chip bonding to driver ICs and simplifying high-volume assembly.

Equally critical is the fiber interface that determines optical I/O density and cost. In conventional designs, each VCSEL couples to a single-core fiber with a 125-µm cladding, which limits array pitch and inflates fiber count and connector complexity. Multicore fibers (MCFs) offer an elegant solution: a single 125-µm cladding containing multiple cores (19 in our design) allows compact spatial multiplexing without altering the form factor or manufacturing process.

In this work, we demonstrate a co-packaged optical chiplet that integrates BSE VCSEL and backsideilluminated photodiode (PD) arrays directly with 19-core MCFs. Each VCSEL aligns to an individual fiber core, achieving high-density, low-loss coupling without active alignment. The VCSEL arrays feature 43-µm pitch emitters and are flip-chip bonded to electronic ICs (EICs) containing drivers and TIAs. Multiple 19-channel arrays on a single 1.6 mm × 0.6 mm die enable 57-channel configurations with sub-2 pJ/bit energy. # 2. Device Design and Architecture

As illustrated in Fig. [1,](#page-1-0) the optical engine is a heterogeneous multi-die assembly that combines a high-speed electronic IC (EIC) with optical dies (VCSEL and PD arrays) and an optical interface stack, all co-integrated with a dummy-silicon block that ties directly to the package lid. The EIC furnishes the VCSEL driver and TIA functions and routes signals to the substrate through solder microbumps, while the dummy-silicon establishes a dominant vertical heat path from the optical dies to the lid. This vertical conduction mitigates lateral spreading through lower-conductivity adhesives and ferrules and simultaneously stiffens the stack to protect the microlens/FOP assembly during connector insertion.

The transmit array consists of GaAs backside-emitting VCSELs with DBR mirrors and 2–5 µm oxide apertures designed for 940–1060 nm emission. Each subarray contains 19 emitters on a 43 µm pitch matched to the 19-core MCF geometry; three subarrays placed on a 540 µm grid fit within a 1.6 mm × 0.6 mm die footprint to realize 57 channels per chiplet. Emission through the substrate decouples the optical aperture from metal pad routing and enables clean Cu-pillar flip-chip bonding to the EIC without a transparent interposer.

On the receive side, GaAs photodiodes are illuminated from the backside and incorporate etched microlenses (30–50 µm diameter, 5–20 µm height) that concentrate light from each fiber core onto a smaller active area, improving coupling efficiency while reducing capacitance and preserving bandwidth. A fiber-optical plate is bonded above the lens field with an index-matched adhesive (*n* = 1.4–1.8). A raised protective topography around each lens cavity limits adhesive creep and preserves the optical clear aperture during assembly and repeated socketing.

The EIC, implemented in a 28 nm CMOS process optimized for high-speed current-mode logic, connects to the VCSEL/PD dies via Cu pillars at 40–150 µm pitch and 25–40 µm height. The short, low-inductance interconnect and controlled pad capacitance extend electrical bandwidth beyond 10 GHz, enabling 25–50 Gb/s NRZ operation. Through-silicon vias within the EIC provide compact vertical routing to the substrate, minimizing loop area and easing power distribution and return-path design.

Coupling to the 19-core MCF uses a precision ferrule with dual alignment pins and mechanical hard stops. The core-to-core spacing of 43 µm is one-to-one with the VCSEL/PD lattice, allowing passive array alignment at assembly-line speeds. Tolerance analysis and measurements indicate that lateral offsets of ±5 µm and tilts of ±0.05◦ introduce less than 1 dB additional loss, which is compatible with high-yield OSAT processes.

Thermally and mechanically, the dummy-silicon bridge conducts more than 80% of the heat to the lid and optional vapor-chamber elements, keeping the EIC-to-lid temperature gradient below 15◦C at 2 W optical-engine power and maintaining VCSEL junctions below 70◦C. This limits wavelength drift to <0.1 nm/◦C and improves long-term reliability. The same element serves as a stiffener that limits die warpage and safeguards the lens/FOP assembly during fiber insertion. High-conductivity underfills and tuned TIM thickness further balance CTE stress with thermal resistance. The overall stack height—EIC, VCSEL/PD dies, dummy-Si, FOP, and ferrule—remains below 1 mm, making the engine compatible with standard GPU package envelopes and enabling modular scaling from a single 19-channel subarray to 38- and 57-channel variants tiled around a GPU.

## 3. Results and Discussion

First-batch BSE VCSEL chips and 19-core MCFs were fabricated and evaluated on ceramic interposers that replicate the intended optical-engine footprint and provide low-inductance RF probe access. The I–V curves are repeatable across channels, indicating uniform epitaxy and stable flip-chip interfaces; the mild rolloff above 9 mA is consistent with self-heating in the uncooled benchtop configuration. Small-signal measurements (Fig. [2d](#page-2-3)) exhibit *S*<sup>21</sup> –3 dB bandwidths beyond 10 GHz across multiple die locations, sufficient for 25–50 Gb/s NRZ. The frequency response is smooth and largely free of peaking, reflecting well-controlled impedance and low parasitics from the Cu-pillar interconnect; fits to the data suggest an effective device capacitance below 0.2 pF, in line with the designed oxide aperture.

End-to-end fiber-coupled tests yield a preliminary link budget of approximately 7.5 dB: about 3 dB from fiber attenuation at the test length, 2 dB from VCSEL-to-fiber coupling, 0.5 dB across the connector pair, and 2 dB from fiber-to-PD coupling. At the receiver, the resulting power of roughly –6 dBm together with 0.5 A/W PD responsivity provides adequate SNR for 25 Gb/s NRZ with standard FEC. Process refinements—including microlens curvature tuning, adhesive index control, and ferrule tolerance trimming—are expected to recover an additional 1–2 dB. Receiver tests confirm >10 GHz bandwidth and dark current <1 nA at 2 V reverse bias. The microlens/FOP assembly improves coupling by about 2 dB relative to flat-back diodes and maintains responsivity within specification for ±5 µm lateral misalignment, supporting passive array alignment without per-channel active tuning. Energy and density estimates derived from the measured efficiencies and nominal driver swings indicate sub-2 pJ/bit at 25 Gb/s per channel. With 19 channels in a 1.6 mm × 0.6 mm footprint, the areal bandwidth density exceeds 0.5 Tb/s/mm<sup>2</sup> ; three subarrays per chiplet deliver more than 1.4 Tb/s aggregate, enabling rack- or pod-level deployments that surpass 100 Tb/s with conservative fan-out.

Beyond basic optical and electrical validation, the demonstrated chiplet architecture also provides a clear path toward large-scale co-packaged deployment. The measured 10 GHz modulation bandwidth supports 25–50 Gb/s NRZ operation, but optimization of the driver peaking network and oxide aperture geometry is expected to extend the –3 dB bandwidth beyond 15 GHz, enabling 100 Gb/s PAM4 modulation. Preliminary eye-diagram simulations using the measured *S*<sup>21</sup> data confirm open eyes at 50 Gb/s NRZ and 100 Gb/s PAM4 with standard feed-forward equalization, suggesting that the current device can already serve as a building block for next-generation 800G and 1.6T transceivers.

Thermal and system-level tests confirm the robustness and scalability of the architecture. Cycling between 25◦C and 85◦C for 200 rounds caused no L–I–V degradation, verifying the reliability of the Cu-pillar and dummy-silicon heat path. Simulations show hotspots below 90◦C under full 57-channel load. A GPU package with twenty such chiplets can exceed 100 Tb/s bandwidth within a 50 mm × 50 mm footprint. Compared with silicon-photonics CPO engines, the VCSEL–MCF design removes external lasers and active alignment, achieving <1 pJ/bit efficiency and lower assembly cost. These optical, thermal, and economic advantages outline a manufacturable path toward fully integrated optical compute packages for future AI systems.

In summary, the demonstrated MCF-coupled backside-emitting VCSEL/PD chiplet achieves excellent optical, thermal, and mechanical characteristics and offers a manufacturable route toward multi-Tb/s co-packaged optical interconnects for next-generation GPU systems.
