# A 1.6 Tbit/s WDM Integrated Photonic IMDD Transmitter on Thin-Film Lithium Tantalate

Abstract: We demonstrate a fully integrated photonic IMDD transmitter on a monolithic thin-film lithium tantalate platform, achieving an aggregate net data rate of 1.6 Tbit/s using wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM) with PAM-4 and PAM-8 modulation formats.

### 1. Introduction

The rapid rise of data-intensive applications in artificial intelligence, cloud computing, and high-performance data centers has created an urgent demand for compact, energy-efficient optical interconnects. As switch ASICs now approach 51.2 Tbit/s bandwidth [\[1\]](#page-2-0), the limits of electrical interconnects have become a critical bottleneck. Copackaged optics (CPO), where optical engines are integrated directly with electronic switches, is emerging as the only viable path forward [\[2\]](#page-2-1). Achieving this vision requires scalable, multi-terabit transmitters capable of routing and modulating hundreds of wavelengths in a compact footprint. Our work addresses this need by demonstrating the first integrated AWG-modulator-AWG transmitter on lithium tantalate on insulator (LTOI). The device features a single input and single output waveguide, providing a simple, scalable, and energy-efficient architecture that delivers 1.6 Tbit/s aggregate net throughput on a single compact chip. This platform unites broadband spectral routing and high-speed electro-optic modulation, representing an essential step toward co-packaged, terabit-class optical data center interconnects for next-generation computing systems.

## 2. Large-capacity optical transmitter based on thin-film lithium tantalate

Arrayed waveguide gratings (AWGs) are fundamental building blocks in photonic integrated circuits [\[3,](#page-2-2) [4\]](#page-2-3), performing wavelength-selective routing with high precision. To exploit these individual wavelengths for data transmission, the first AWG (demultiplexer) plays a crucial role of separating the incoming multi-wavelength optical source into discrete optical carriers, each directed to its own output waveguide. While Ref. [\[5\]](#page-2-4) demonstrated AWGs on lithium tantalate-on-insulator (LTOI) with 100 GHz channel spacing, Ref. [\[6\]](#page-2-5) reported the integration of an AWG with electro-optic modulators for wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM). However, no prior work has realized a complete AWG-modulator-AWG system, where two AWGs are lithographically aligned and interconnected through on-chip modulators – a task that demands sub-micron precision, especially on anisotropic ferroelectric materials such as lithium niobate. Building on the recent demonstration of dual aligned AWGs on LTOI [\[5\]](#page-2-4), we integrate electro-optic modulators between them, enabling per-channel data modulation within a monolithic lithium tantalate platform. The resulting device, with a single input and single output waveguide, represents the first Demultiplexer-Modulator-Multiplexer transmitter on lithium tantalate, combining broadband spectral routing and high-speed modulation for scalable integrated WDM systems.

#### 3. High-speed data communication experiment

We demonstrate the performance of the designed and fabricated eight-channel LTOI WDM transmitter in a high-speed data communication experiment, see Fig. [2\(](#page-1-0)a) for the associated setup. To account for inter-channel crosstalk, two WDM channels are tested simultaneously. Two external cavity lasers (ECLs) are combined using a polarization-maintaining coupler to serve as multi-wavelength optical carriers, which are fed into the LTOI WDMtransmitter chip. On chip, the first AWG separates the optical carriers, and each carrier is individually modulated by a Mach-Zehnder modulator (MZM) biased at quadrature point. Two channels of a high-speed digital-to-analog converter (DAC, M8199B, Keysight) synthesize electrical drive signals, which are amplified and applied to the MZM coplanar waveguide (CPW) electrodes through a 110 GHz ground-signal-ground-ground-signal-ground (GSGGSG) RF probe. The modulated optical signals from two WDM channels are recombined into a single waveguide by a second AWG, aligned precisely with the first one, and transmitted over fiber. At the receiver, the WDM signal is demultiplexed by an programmable filter (WaveShaper 15000S, Finisar) for simplicity. An erbiumdoped fiber amplifier (EDFA) is placed before the demultiplexer to ensure sufficient input power of 10 dBm for the high-speed photodiode (PD). This EDFA can be omitted in practical systems with electrical amplification after the PD. The electrical waveform generated at the PD output is captured by a real-time oscilloscope (RTO, UXR series, Keysight) and processed by offline digital signal processing (DSP), including resampling to 2Sa/symbol, timing recovery and linear equalization.

In the experiments, 80 GBd PAM8 and 100 GBd PAM4 formats are demonstrated, both pulse-shaped by digital root-raised cosine filters with a roll-off factors of 0.05 and 0.01, respectively. The corresponding optical spectra of the eight-channel WDM signals spanning carrier frequencies from *f*<sup>1</sup> = 192.517 THz to *f*<sup>8</sup> = 193.259 THz with exactly 106 GHz spacing, are shown in Fig. [2\(](#page-1-0)b) and (e). Each spectrum merges four measurement sets (colorcoded), each with two neighboring channels operated simultaneously. The recovered eye diagrams from four measurement sets covering all eight channels are displayed in Fig. [2](#page-1-0) (c) and (f). The corresponding achievable information rate (AIR) and net data rate (NDR) across the WDM carrier frequencies *f*1... *f*<sup>8</sup> are shown in Fig. [2](#page-1-0) (d) and (g). All channels exhibit bit error ratios (BER) well below the 25% SD-FEC threshold. An aggregate net date rate of 1.6943 Tbit/s is achieved with the eight-channel 80 GBd PAM8 WDM signal, clearly indicating the potential of our fully integrated LTOI WDM transmitter for next-generation large-capacity optical interconnects.

#### 4. Summary

We demonstrate a 1.6 Tbit/s wavelength-division multiplexed photonic transmitter on a monolithic thin-film lithium tantalate (TFLT) platform. The device integrates two lithographically aligned AWGs with an eight-channel Mach-Zehnder modulator array, achieving high-speed and low-loss operation. This architecture showcases the potential of lithium tantalate for compact, scalable, and energy-efficient optical transmitters. Looking forward, such integrated electro-optic systems can enable co-packaged terabit-class optical interconnects for next-generation data centers and computing hardware.
