All-dielectric integrated microlens coupler for scalable and efficient photonic I/Os
Sirui Tang,1,\ Jianheng Luo, 1 Johannes Henriksson, 1 Yiyang Zhi, 1 Daniel Klawson, 1 Arkadev Roy, 1 Huicong Deng, 1 and Ming C. Wu 1 
1 Department of EECS, University of California, Berkeley, Berkeley, California, 94720 \ sirui tang@berkeley.edu 
Abstract: We develop a wafer-scale SiON process for all-dielectric integrated microlens couplers, enabling efficient, broadband, polarization-insensitive, and environmentally robust fiber-to-chip coupling with 1.0 dB measured loss.
1. Introduction
As AI systems scale rapidly, the demand for high-speed and energy-efficient data transfer is driving the adoption of optical interconnects [\[1\]](#page-2-0). While silicon photonics has been widely used, achieving efficient and scalable fiberto-chip coupling remains a major challenge for mass production [\[2\]](#page-2-1). Conventional methods of edge coupling and grating coupling each have inherent limitations. Grating couplers suffer from bandwidth and polarization sensitivity and are susceptible to fabrication variations, whereas edge couplers require complex spot size conversion, substrate etching, and facet preparation. In recent years, there are renewed interests in free-space micro-optic coupling. Various approaches, such as serially printed lenses on edge facets and integrated microlens couplers (IMCs) formed by thermal reflow of photoresist, have been reported \[2–[4\]](#page-2-2). These methods maintain high coupling efficiency under relaxed alignment tolerances. However, the use of polymer materials has low thermal budget, which limits the durability and reliability of such couplers in the packaging processes, where the polymer lenses can soften or degrade [\[4\]](#page-2-2). Thus, commercial adoption of polymer-based IMCs has been restricted.
In this work, we introduce a novel wafer-scale, all-dielectric IMC platform based on silicon oxynitride (SiON). The IMC elevates light from photonic integrated circuit (PIC) waveguides, expands its mode size, and collimates it into free space for efficient coupling to single-mode fibers or larger mode field beams. We developed plasmaenhanced chemical vapor deposition (PECVD) processes for depositing ultra-thick, low-stress SiON films and anisotropic etching recipes for transferring the reflowed polymer microlens patterns into SiON, enabling the fabrication of precisely shaped spherical dielectric microlenses on chip. We present the design, simulation, and fabrication of the proposed SiON-based IMC and demonstrate fiber-to-waveguide coupling loss of 1.0 dB in O-band and 1.4 dB in C-band, with polarization-dependent losses (PDL) of 0.3 dB and 0.1 dB, respectively.

2. Design and Simulation
The device comprises a SiON waveguide that transfers light from the PIC waveguide into a SiON microlens via inter-layer adiabatic coupling, as shown in Fig. 1. Within the lens, the light undergoes total internal reflection (TIR) at the SiON–oxide interface, bending upward and expanding in mode size as it propagates through the microlens before being collimated by the spherical surface. The key advantage of this design lies in the upward expansion of light, which lifts the beam and allows it to expand freely inside the lens. This feature allows edge coupling without dependence of the mode size on the buried oxide thickness, eliminating the need for additional facet processing such as silicon substrate etching. The refractive coupling structure is inherently broadband and polarization-insensitive, providing large alignment tolerances. Moreover, the lens dimensions can be tailored to balance specific translational and rotational tolerance requirements.
Fig. 1 includes the finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) simulation of light propagation within an optimized IMC coupled to a single-mode fiber. The microlens has a height of 18.1 µm, a radius of 20.4 µm, and a refractive index of 1.57. Simulations predict an optimal coupling loss as low as 0.34 dB for TE mode and 0.32 dB for TM mode when a 250-nm-thick MgF 2 (n = 1.39) anti-reflection coating is applied to the lens surface.

3. Fabrication
The primary challenges in developing SiON microlenses are achieving ultra-thick (>20 µm) SiON deposition to match the lens height and establishing a scalable, accurate process for forming spherical lens structures. To address these challenges, both the deposition and pattern transfer processes were developed and optimized.
SiON was selected for its tunable refractive index. Varying the PECVD N2:N2O flow ratio modifies the oxide– nitride composition, thereby controlling the index. We observed that this ratio also has a strong influence on film stress. Since oxide and nitride films exhibit opposite intrinsic stresses, an optimal gas ratio exists where the net film stress approaches zero. By fine-tuning the N 2 and N2O flows, we identified conditions that minimize wafer bow. We then adjusted the total gas flow to refine the index near this low-stress point, and by increasing the total flow aligned the minimum-stress condition with the target index of 1.57. Experimental results confirm that SiON layers exceeding 20 µm in thickness can be deposited with wafer bowing below 10 µm.
To fabricate hemispherical SiON microlenses, we employed a pattern transfer technique that replicates thermally reflowed photoresist profiles onto the SiON layer via anisotropic etching. Oxygen was introduced into the CF4/CHF 3 plasma to adjust the etch selectivity relative to the reflowed photoresist (AZ 40XT). By optimizing oxygen flow, a 1:1 etch selectivity between AZ 40XT and SiON was achieved, enabling faithful shape transfer.
The fabrication process is shown in Fig. 2. It begins with the deposition of a SiON layer onto an oxide-packaged chip, forming the waveguide layer for optical coupling. A silicon hard mask is then patterned to define the SiON waveguide. Next, a 20 µm-thick low-stress SiON layer is deposited and planarized via chemical-mechanical polishing to serve as the microlens material. A disk pattern is defined on the top surface to limit the microlens radius by surface tension during thermal reflow. The AZ 40XT photoresist is then coated, patterned, and thermally reflowed to form hemispherical lenses. Finally, the lens profile is transferred into the SiON layer through anisotropic etching with near-unity selectivity, followed by SiON waveguide etching and silicon hard-mask removal.

5. Conclusion
We propose and experimentally demonstrate a wafer-scale optical packaging approach based on dielectric integrated microlens couplers (IMCs). Free-space coupling using SiON IMCs delivers low coupling loss (0.32 dB simulated, 1.0 dB measured), broadband operation (both O- and C-bands), low polarization-dependence, large alignment tolerance (±2 µm lateral), compact footprint, and high environmental robustness. Coupled with the fact that our IMC is compatible with any standard silicon-photonics platforms, this work delivers a practical route to a robust, scalable, and detachable fiber-to-chip connector.
