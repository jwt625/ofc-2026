# **O-band Membrane Surface-Emitting Laser on a Si Substrate Demonstrating 100-Gbps PAM-4 Operation**

**Takuma Tsurugaya<sup>1</sup> , Yoshiho Maeda<sup>1</sup> , Nikolaos-Panteleimon Diamantopoulos<sup>1</sup> , Takuma Aihara<sup>1</sup> , Takuro Fujii<sup>1</sup> , Erina Kanno<sup>1</sup> , Tomonari Sato<sup>1</sup> , Fumio Koyama<sup>2</sup> , and Shinji Matsuo<sup>1</sup>**

*<sup>1</sup>Device Technology Labs., NTT, Inc., 3-1 Morinosato-Wakamiya, Atsugi, Kanagawa, 243–0198, Japan <sup>2</sup>Institute of Integrated Research, Institute of Science Tokyo, 4259 Nagatsuta, Midori-ku, Yokohama, Kanagawa, 226-8503, Japan takuma.tsurugaya@ntt.com* 

**Abstract:** We demonstrate an O-band surface-emitting laser on a Si substrate, based on an inplane membrane distributed-reflector laser with a second-order surface grating, exhibiting a high modulation efficiency and 100-Gbps PAM-4 operation over 2-km SSMF. © 2025 The Authors

### **1. Introduction**

Surface-emitting lasers (SELs) in the O/C-bands hold great potential for short- to long-reach interconnects that meet the ever-increasing datacenter traffic demands, owing to their two-dimensional (2D) ultra-dense integrability and the low-loss, low-dispersion transmission advantages of these bands. While considerable progress has been made in the O/C-band VCSELs [1-4], they still face challenges inherent to InP-based materials [1,2]. Specifically, their modulation bandwidths typically remain around 10 GHz [1,2], with the highest reported values of 17 GHz [3] and 21 GHz [4] for the O- and C-bands, in contrast to the GaAs-based shorter-band VCSELs reaching up to 50 GHz [5].

In this context, we recently proposed "membrane surface-emitting lasers" [6], comprising a thin InP layer with first- and second-order surface gratings acting as a distributed Bragg reflector (DBR) and a grating coupler (GC) for in-plane cavity formation and grating-coupled surface emission, respectively. The membrane structure surrounded by low refractive index materials exhibits strong optical confinement into the buried active region, which is a key feature for high-efficiency, high-speed direct modulation; our membrane edge-emitting lasers (EELs) have achieved modulation bandwidths of ~25 GHz on Si substrates [7,8] and up to 108 GHz on SiC substrates with a photonphoton-resonance (PPR) effect [9]. The membrane structure with surface gratings provides a high coupling constant and a high out-of-plane radiation constant for the first- and second-order gratings, respectively, enabling short-cavity lasers with low power consumption [10] and high-efficiency grating-coupled surface emission [6].

In [6], we fabricated membrane SELs on a six-pair TiO2/SiO2 bottom reflector formed on a Si substrate to ensure high bottom reflectance. However, the >2-μm-thick reflector impeded efficient heat dissipation to the Si substrate, which limited the maximum injection current and thus hindered high-speed direct modulation. In this work, we improve the heat dissipation by reducing the distance between the laser and the Si substrate, through a newly designed ~560-nm-thick amorphous-Si (a-Si)/SiO₂ intermediate reflective structure that maintains a high upward radiation efficiency. The device exhibits a 9.1-GHz/mA1/2 modulation current efficiency and a 25-GHz modulation bandwidth, and demonstrates 100-Gbps PAM-4 operation over 2-km standard single-mode fiber (SSMF).

As shown in Fig. 1(b), the active sections consist of a 220-nm-thick InP membrane containing a buried active region made of InGaAlAs-based nine quantum wells with a lateral-current-injection (LCI) structure, while the passive sections consist of InP channel waveguides butt-coupled to the active section. The intermediate stack between the InP membrane and Si substrate consists of a top SiO2 (TOX) layer, an a-Si layer, and a buried SiO2 (BOX) layer with thicknesses of *t*TOX, *t*a-Si, and *t*BOX, respectively. The a-Si and BOX thicknesses were set by the quarter-wave condition *t* = *λ*/(4*n*) using the measured refractive index of a-Si (*n*a-Si = 2.97), yielding *t*a-Si = 110 nm and *t*BOX = 225 nm. The upward radiation efficiency *η*up of the membrane GC structure was calculated as a function of *t*TOX using the finite-difference time-domain (FDTD) method, as shown in Fig. 1(c). A maximum *η*up of 91% was obtained at *t*TOX = 225 nm, even though the bottom reflectance *R*bot of the a-Si/BOX/Si-substrate stack was only ~67%. The oscillatory dependence on *t*TOX indicates that constructive interference is harnessed to obtain a high *η*up even with a moderate *R*bot. In combination with employing a-Si, which has a higher refractive index than TiO2, this design allows a reduction in the bottom-reflector thickness from [6] while maintaining a high *η*up.

The detailed design of the longitudinal cavity structure is shown in Fig. 1(d). Between the DFB sections lies our proposed "Gaussian-phase-apodized grating coupler" (GPA-GC) [6], in which the grating phase profile is apodized to produce a Gaussian radiation profile with a desired mode-field diameter (MFD), with an appropriate cavity-mode standing wave in phase with the GC unit cells. When the MFD is set well below the GPA-GC length, this design ensures single-mode lasing of the Gaussian-radiating mode, because other modes radiate throughout the GPA-GC section and thus incur much higher losses. The target MFD is set to 9.2 μm to facilitate SSMF coupling. Figures 1(e) and (f) present FDTD-calculated radiation profiles for the SEL cavity with a 40-μm-long GPA-GC section, confirming the intended Gaussian-like upward beam with only minor downward leakage into the Si substrate.

For heterogeneously fabricating the membrane SELs, we employed the micro-transfer-printing method [8]; the membrane SEL structures were fabricated on an InP substrate, with an underlying sacrificial layer and an ~400-nmthick SiON overcladding layer, whereas the BOX, a-Si, and TOX layers were deposited on a Si substrate to realize the designed bottom-reflector structure. Subsequently, through resist-tether formation and sacrificial-layer removal, the individual SEL coupons were transfer-printed onto the TOX surface of the Si substrate.

#### **3. Device Characterization and Eye Measurement**

We measured a fabricated membrane SEL with a 40-μm-long GPA-GC section, 15-μm-long DFB sections (total active length *L*a = 70 μm), and 40-μm-long DBR sections terminated with InP inverse tapers. All measurements were performed at a stage temperature of 25℃. Figure 2(a) shows the L-I-V characteristics measured with the spatial photodiode (PD) setup, where a spatial PD with 9.5-mm-φ input aperture was placed ~5 mm above the membrane SEL to detect upward output power. A threshold current of 1.6 mA, a near-threshold slope efficiency of 0.29 W/A (inset of Fig. 2(a)), and a maximum output power of 1.88 mW at 13 mA were obtained. This slope efficiency is notably higher than those of buried-heterostructure grating-coupled SELs (e.g., 0.075 W/A [11]). Further increase in the slope efficiency is expected by optimizing the grating etch depth to obtain a higher radiation constant in the GC section. Figure 2(b) shows lasing spectra measured with the SSMF setup, where the cleaved facet of an SSMF (SMF-28e+, Corning) was vertically coupled to the GPA-GC section. Single-mode lasing was obtained over the entire current range with a high side-mode suppression ratio (SMSR) of 54 dB at 13 mA, indicating that the DR cavity stably supported an appropriate cavity mode. Finally, we performed dynamic characterization and eye-diagram measurement at 25℃, by using the SSMF setup, with a praseodymium-doped fiber amplifier (PDFA) to compensate for the SSMF coupling loss. Figure 3(a) shows the electro-optic (E/O) small-signal responses, exhibiting a maximum 3-dB modulation bandwidth *f*3dB of 25.0 GHz at 15 mA. To the best of our knowledge, this modulation bandwidth represents a record-high value for O/C-band SELs. Figure 3(b) plots *f*3dB from the E/O responses and the relaxation oscillation frequency *fr* from the relative intensity noise (RIN) measurement, versus square root of the bias current above threshold, yielding a modulation current efficiency factor (MCEF) of 9.1 GHz/mA1/2 and a D-factor of 7.1 GHz/mA1/2. Figures 2(c) and (d) present non-return-to-zero (NRZ) eye diagrams at 40 and 50 Gbps for a 2<sup>31</sup>-1 pseudo-random bit sequence (PRBS) generated by a pulse-pattern generator (PPG) under a back-to-back (BTB) setup without any equalization. The bias current and the signal's peak-to-peak voltage were 13 mA and 0.6 V, respectively. Clear eye openings with extinction ratios (ERs) over 2 dB confirm 50-GBaud-class modulation capability. Figures 2(e)-(h) show PAM-4 eyes at 80 and 100 Gbps for a 2<sup>13</sup>-1 PRBS generated by an arbitrary-waveform generator (AWG), evaluated in BTB and 2-km SSMF transmission setups, using a 5-tap feed-forward equalizer (FFE) in the receiver sampling oscilloscope. The bias current and the signal's peak-to-peak voltage were 13 mA and 1.2 V, respectively. All cases exhibit eye openings with outer ERs exceeding 4 dB, demonstrating up to 100-Gbps PAM-4 operation over 2-km SSMF. At this bias current, the device consumes 32.9 mW, yielding energy costs of 411 fJ/bit and 329 fJ/bit for the 80- and 100-Gbps PAM-4 operations, respectively.

# **4. Conclusion**

In summary, we have demonstrated an O-band membrane distributed-reflector SEL on a Si substrate incorporating a thin a-Si/SiO₂ intermediate layer that effectively serves as a bottom reflector and a heat sink. The device achieves an MCEF of 9.1 GHz/mA1/2, reaches an *f*3dB of 25 GHz, and demonstrates 100-Gbps PAM-4 transmission over 2-km SSMF. These results highlight the high-modulation-efficiency advantage of membrane SELs and their promise as high-speed, 2D-arrayable transmitters for ultra-high-capacity short- to long-reach optical interconnects.
